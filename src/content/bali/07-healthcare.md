# Chapter 7: Healthcare & Insurance

> **Bali's healthcare is a tale of two systems.** Private international hospitals are excellent. Public facilities are not designed for foreigners. The gap is enormous — and your insurance is what determines which side you land on.

---

## The Two-Tier System

| | Private/International | Public |
|---|---|---|
| **Quality** | International standard | Basic |
| **English** | Yes | Limited |
| **Cost** | Moderate to high | Very low |
| **Wait times** | Short | Long |
| **Who uses it** | Foreigners, wealthy locals | Local population |
| **Insurance** | International insurance accepted | BPJS (local insurance) |

**Rule of thumb:** For anything beyond a minor issue, go private. The price difference is worth it.

## Best Hospitals for Foreigners

### Denpasar / South Bali

**BIMC Hospital Nusa Dua** ⭐ Top Pick
- International accreditation (JCI pending)
- 24-hour emergency department
- Direct billing with major international insurers
- English-speaking staff
- Location: Nusa Dua
- Emergency: (0361) 761 263

**Siloam Hospitals Bali**
- JCI accredited
- Centre of Excellence: Cardiology, Emergency & Trauma, Orthopaedics
- Modern facilities
- Location: Kuta/Denpasar
- Emergency: (0361) 779 900

**Bali International Hospital (BIH)** — New (2025)
- World-class facility in Sanur Special Economic Zone
- Specialized: cardiology, oncology, neurology, gastroenterology
- 20-room emergency department
- Aiming to be a regional medical tourism hub

**Prima Medika Hospital**
- Location: Kuta
- Specialties: cardiology, neurology, orthopedics, pediatrics
- Good for non-emergency specialist visits

### Ubud
- **No major international hospital** — nearest is BIMC (45-60 min drive)
- Local clinics handle minor issues
- For anything serious: go to Denpasar
- This is a real consideration when choosing Ubud as your base

### Mobile Clinics
- **Trishnanda Care Centre** — House calls with transparent pricing, medication delivery. Great for non-emergency issues when you don't want to travel to a hospital.

### Our Recommendation

**Most nomads in Bali:** **Genki Traveler**

Why? The motorbike clause. In Bali, you **will** ride a motorbike. Most insurance companies require a valid motorcycle license from your home country for claims. Genki covers scooters ≤125cc (the standard rental scooter) without requiring a motorcycle-specific license — you just need a helmet. This single detail can save you thousands.

**Alternative:** SafetyWing Essential ($56/4 weeks) if you have a proper motorcycle license or don't plan to ride.

**Staying 1+ year:** Pacific Cross or Cigna Global for comprehensive coverage with direct hospital billing.

### The Motorbike Insurance Truth

> ⚠️ **This is the #1 insurance claim denial in Bali.** Most travel insurance policies require:
> 1. A valid motorcycle license (from your home country or IDP)
> 2. A proper helmet worn at the time of accident
> 3. No alcohol involvement
>
> Without ALL THREE, your claim will be denied. A broken leg from a motorbike accident at a private hospital can cost $3,000-10,000+. Get your IDP before you leave home, or choose Genki.

## BPJS — Indonesia's National Health Insurance

**Who can get it:** Foreign nationals with KITAS/KITAP who've resided in Indonesia 6+ consecutive months.

**Cost:** ~$10-25 USD/month depending on class (Class 1 = best rooms)

**What it covers:** GP visits, emergency treatment, hospitalization in public facilities, basic medications, specialist referrals.

**Reality check:** Covers public hospitals only. Quality is acceptable for routine care but not comparable to private international hospitals. Most expats who have BPJS also maintain private insurance.

**For most digital nomads:** Not relevant (requires KITAS + 6 months residence). Stick with international insurance.

## Common Health Risks in Bali

### Dengue Fever 🦟
- Spread by daytime mosquitoes
- Symptoms: high fever, severe headache, joint pain, rash
- No vaccine widely available (Dengvaxia exists but limited)
- **Prevention:** Mosquito repellent (DEET-based), long sleeves at dusk/dawn, mosquito coils in your villa
- Treatment: rest, fluids, paracetamol (NOT aspirin or ibuprofen)

### Bali Belly (Traveler's Diarrhea)
- Extremely common in first 1-2 weeks
- Cause: new bacteria in food/water
- **Prevention:** Avoid tap water (even for brushing teeth), be cautious with ice at street stalls, wash hands frequently
- Treatment: rehydration salts (available at any pharmacy), bland diet, usually resolves in 2-3 days

### Motorbike Injuries
- The #1 health risk for nomads in Bali
- Road rash, fractures, head injuries
- **Prevention:** Helmet always, defensive riding, no alcohol, proper license
- Insurance coverage: see motorbike section above

### Rabies
- Present in Bali (dogs and monkeys)
- If bitten: clean wound immediately with soap and water, go to hospital for post-exposure prophylaxis
- Consider pre-exposure vaccination before your trip

### Sun/Heat
- Tropical sun is intense — wear sunscreen daily (SPF 50+)
- Stay hydrated (3+ liters/day)
- Heat exhaustion symptoms: dizziness, nausea, headache → get to shade, drink water, cool down

## Pharmacy

Indonesian pharmacies (apotek) are relatively relaxed:

### Available Without Prescription
- Antibiotics (amoxicillin, ciprofloxacin)
- Antihistamines
- Anti-diarrhea medication
- Pain relievers
- Oral contraceptives
- Basic anti-malarial medication

### Requires Prescription
- Controlled substances (benzos, opioids, ADHD medication)
- Some specialized antibiotics

> ⚠️ **Bring your own supply** of any regular medication from home. Availability of specific brands/formulations is not guaranteed.

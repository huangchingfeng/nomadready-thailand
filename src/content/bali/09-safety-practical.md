## Motorbike Safety — #1 Risk

This is not an exaggeration: **motorbike accidents are the leading cause of injury and death among foreigners in Bali.**

### Rules That Save Lives
- **Helmet. Always.** A real one, not a decorative shell. Your insurance requires it.
- **International Driving Permit (IDP)** — get it before you leave home. Police checkpoints are frequent. Fine without license: 500,000 IDR ($32). Insurance denial without license: $3,000-10,000+.
- **No alcohol.** Not even one beer. Bali roads are narrow, unlit, and unpredictable.
- **Ride defensively.** Assume every other vehicle will do something unexpected. They will.
- **Avoid riding at night** — minimal street lighting, drunk drivers, stray dogs
- **Long pants + closed shoes** reduce road rash severity significantly

### If You Have an Accident
1. Get medical help immediately (hospital or call your insurance hotline)
2. Document everything — photos of scene, injuries, damage
3. Get a police report if the accident involves another vehicle
4. Contact your insurance within 24 hours
5. Do NOT sign any documents you don't understand

## Petty Theft & Bag Snatching

### Common Scenarios
- **Bag snatching from motorbike:** Thieves on a passing motorbike grab your bag/phone while you're walking or riding. Most common in Kuta, Seminyak, and busy Canggu streets.
- **Beach theft:** Leave your stuff on the beach while swimming → it's gone
- **Villa break-ins:** Rare but happen, especially in isolated areas

### Prevention
- Wear your bag cross-body on the building side of the road (away from traffic)
- Don't use your phone while walking on busy roads
- Use your villa safe for passport, cash, and electronics
- Lock your motorbike (steering lock + disc lock for expensive bikes)
- Don't leave valuables visible in your motorbike basket

## Scams

### Money Changer Scams
- Short-counting (distraction while counting)
- Hiding bills under the counter
- "No commission" signs that actually have hidden fees
- **Prevention:** Use bank ATMs or reputable licensed exchangers only. Count money at the counter.

### Taxi/Transport Scams
- Inflated meter or "broken meter" at airport
- **Prevention:** Use Grab/Gojek exclusively. Pre-agree price for traditional taxis.

### Villa Rental Scams
- Fake listings on Facebook/Instagram, payment requested via transfer, villa doesn't exist
- **Prevention:** Never pay full rent before seeing the villa in person. Use escrow if paying online.

### ATM Skimming
- Card reader overlays on standalone ATMs
- **Prevention:** Use ATMs inside bank branches or malls. Cover your PIN.

### "Tourist Price"
- Being charged 2-5x the local price for goods and services
- **Prevention:** Learn approximate prices (this guide helps). Ask "berapa?" (how much?) and negotiate.

## Water Safety

### Drinking Water
- **Do NOT drink tap water.** Ever. Not even in hotels.
- Use bottled water or refill stations (gallon refills available everywhere: 5,000-8,000 IDR)
- Ice in restaurants and cafes: generally safe (made from purified water). Street stalls: use judgment.
- Brush teeth with bottled water for your first week.

### Ocean Safety
- Bali's beaches have **strong currents and undertow**, especially during wet season
- Red flags = do not swim. Seriously.
- Surf only at spots appropriate for your level. Beginners: Kuta Beach, Batu Bolong
- Never surf or swim alone at isolated beaches
- Jellyfish season varies — locals know. Ask before entering water.

## Natural Disasters

### Earthquakes
- Bali is on the Pacific Ring of Fire
- Minor tremors are common and usually harmless
- Major earthquakes: rare but possible (2018 Lombok earthquake affected Bali)
- **Preparation:** Know your building exits. Move away from glass and heavy objects during shaking. If near coast after a strong earthquake, move to higher ground (tsunami risk).

### Volcanic Activity
- Mount Agung (northeast Bali) is an active volcano
- Last major eruption: 2017-2018 (disrupted flights)
- Monitor: BMKG (Indonesian meteorology agency) or Magma Indonesia app
- Airport closures possible during eruptions — have a flexible flight booking

### Flooding (Wet Season)
- Wet season: November - March
- Roads flood quickly during heavy rain, especially in Canggu
- Rice fields turn into swimming pools
- **Impact on work:** Power outages more common. Have a backup plan (power bank, mobile hotspot)

## Drugs — Zero Tolerance

> 🚨 **Indonesia has some of the strictest drug laws in the world.** This is not an exaggeration.

- **Marijuana/cannabis:** Illegal. Possession can result in **4-12 years imprisonment**
- **Hard drugs:** Trafficking carries the **death penalty**
- **Magic mushrooms:** Sold openly at some tourist spots but still **illegal**
- Don't accept anything from strangers
- Don't carry anything through customs for anyone
- Police conduct random checks, especially in Kuta and Canggu nightlife areas

**This is not Thailand where the laws recently softened (and then re-tightened). Indonesia has never wavered. The risk is real.**

## Practical Information

### Electricity
- **230V, 50Hz** — European-style Type C/F plugs
- US/UK travelers need an adapter
- Power outages: 1-3 per month on average, more during wet season
- UPS (uninterruptible power supply) available at electronics stores if your work can't tolerate interruptions

### Laundry
- Laundry by the kilo: **7,000-10,000 IDR/kg ($0.45-0.65)**
- Same-day or next-day service
- Available everywhere — just ask your accommodation
- Ironing usually included

### Grocery Shopping
- **Local markets:** Cheapest, freshest produce. Arrive early morning.
- **Bintang Supermarket:** Western products at reasonable prices. Multiple locations.
- **Pepito Express:** Upscale import groceries. Expensive but comprehensive.
- **Coco Supermarket:** Good middle ground.

### Time Zone
- **WITA (Central Indonesian Time) = GMT+8**
- Same as Singapore, Malaysia, Taipei, Perth
- Works well for: Asian clients/employers
- Challenging for: US West Coast (15-16 hour difference), US East Coast (12-13 hours), UK (8 hours)

### Language
- **Bahasa Indonesia** — the national language. Not Balinese (which is a separate language).
- English is widely spoken in tourist areas
- Learning basic phrases goes a long way:
  - Terima kasih = Thank you
  - Berapa? = How much?
  - Tidak = No
  - Ya = Yes
  - Tolong = Please / Help
  - Permisi = Excuse me

## ATM Strategy

Every Thai ATM charges a **220-250 THB (~$6-7) surcharge** on foreign cards (varies by bank, most now charge 250 THB). No exceptions.

### How to Minimize Fees

**Best: Charles Schwab High Yield Investor Checking**
- Zero foreign transaction fees
- **All ATM surcharges worldwide are reimbursed** — including the 220-250 THB fee
- No monthly fees, no minimum balance
- The gold standard for international nomads

**Good: Wise Debit Card**
- Free ATM withdrawals up to ~$100/month, then 2% fee
- Mid-market exchange rate (0.43-0.6% conversion fee)
- 220-250 THB ATM fee is NOT reimbursed
- Better for card payments than ATM use

**Tip: AEON ATMs** charge only 150 THB (lowest in Thailand). Find them in AEON malls and some 7-Elevens.

### For Non-US Nomads

Charles Schwab is a US-only product. If you don't have a US bank account, here are the best alternatives by region:

**EU/UK Nomads:**
- **Wise (EU IBAN account)** — Fund via SEPA transfer (free/instant), withdraw via Wise debit card in Thailand. Mid-market exchange rate with a small 0.43-0.6% conversion fee. Likely the **best overall option** for EU nomads. Multi-currency account lets you hold EUR, USD, THB, and 50+ currencies.
- **Revolut** — Free ATM withdrawals up to £200/€200 per month (Standard plan), zero FX markup on weekdays. Good exchange rates. Check your plan tier — some don't cover Thai ATM surcharges. Metal/Premium plans increase the free ATM limit.
- **N26** — No foreign transaction fees on card payments. Free ATM withdrawals limited to 3-5 per month (plan-dependent). Good everyday card but not ideal as your primary ATM card in Thailand.
- **DKB (Deutsche Kreditbank)** — Visa debit card with competitive exchange rates. Generally still works after Abmeldung if the account was opened before you left Germany. One of the best German bank cards for international use.
- **Bunq** — Dutch neobank, popular with nomads. Good multi-currency support, metal card option with free international ATM withdrawals. Easy to open for EU residents.

> ⚠️ **Important for Germans:** After Abmeldung, some German banks may restrict services or close your account (especially Sparkasse and Volksbank). **Open your travel-friendly accounts (Wise, Revolut, N26, DKB) BEFORE leaving Germany.** Update your registered address promptly. Keep at least one German account active for insurance premiums, pension contributions, and tax refunds.

**Latin American Nomads:**
- **Nubank** (Brazil) — Zero FX markup on international purchases. Excellent app. ATM withdrawals abroad carry fees.
- **Mercado Pago** — Prepaid card option for Argentina/Mexico. Limited international ATM support but growing.

### Brazilian Nomads: Complete Money Guide

If you're coming from Brazil, your financial setup matters more than most nationalities because of IOF taxes and currency controls. Here's the playbook.

**PIX → Wise: The Cheapest Way to Move Money**
1. Set up a Wise account using your CPF (Cadastro de Pessoa Física)
2. Transfer BRL via PIX to Wise's Brazilian bank account — it's instant and the fee is low (~1.3%)
3. Convert to THB or USD inside Wise at the mid-market rate
4. Use your Wise card for payments and ATM withdrawals in Thailand

This is significantly cheaper than using a Brazilian credit card abroad.

**IOF Tax Warning (Imposto sobre Operações Financeiras)**

| Transaction Type | IOF Rate | Notes |
|-----------------|----------|-------|
| Credit card international purchases | **4.38%** | Added automatically to every transaction |
| International bank transfers (TED/DOC) | 1.1% | Applies to outbound transfers |
| Wise transfers via PIX | **1.1%** | Classified as bank transfer — much cheaper |
| Cash exchange at cambio | 1.1% | Physical currency exchange |

**Strategy:** Use Wise transfers (1.1% IOF) instead of your credit card (4.38% IOF) whenever possible. On $3,500/month, that's a savings of ~$115/month. Over a year, that's $1,380.

**Nubank International**
- Zero FX markup on purchases — one of the best rates from any Brazilian bank
- ATM withdrawals abroad may have fees — use for purchases, not cash
- Keep it as your backup card, not your primary

**C6 Bank Global Account**
- Offers a USD account for Brazilian residents
- Useful if you receive salary in USD — deposit directly, avoid double conversion
- IOF on transfers to C6 Global is 1.1%
- Good option for holding USD without converting to BRL first

**Recommended Nomad Bank Stack**
1. **Wise** (primary) — Transfers from Brazil via PIX, card payments, ATM withdrawals
2. **Nubank** (backup) — Zero-markup purchases, emergency backup
3. **Thai cash reserve** — Keep 5,000-10,000 THB on hand for street food, markets, and emergencies

**Keep Your CPF Active**
Even if you file a Saída Definitiva (see [Chapter 8: Tax Implications](/guide/08-tax-implications)), keep your CPF number active. You'll need it for tax filings, financial operations, and any future transactions with Brazilian institutions. Letting it lapse creates bureaucratic headaches that take months to resolve.

**Asia-Pacific Nomads:**
- **Wise card** — Works everywhere, best mid-market exchange rate, multi-currency account. The universal choice.
- **Revolut** — Strong alternative with competitive rates and free ATM tier.

**The universal pick:** If you can't get Schwab, make Wise your primary card. Budget 220-250 THB ($6-7) per ATM withdrawal as a cost of nomad life. Withdraw larger amounts less frequently (10,000-20,000 THB per withdrawal) to minimize the per-transaction hit.

> ⚠️ **Always decline DCC.** When the ATM asks "charge in your home currency?" — select **NO / WITHOUT CONVERSION**. Accepting DCC costs you 3-5% extra.

For more on managing finances as a nomad, see [Chapter 8: Tax Implications](/guide/08-tax-implications) and [Chapter 2: Cost of Living](/guide/02-cost-of-living).

## US Citizens: FATCA & Banking Reality

If you're American, your banking situation abroad is uniquely complicated. Here's what you need to know.

### Why It's Hard

**FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act)** requires foreign banks to report US account holders to the IRS. The compliance burden is so heavy that many banks worldwide — including some Thai banks — simply refuse to open accounts for Americans rather than deal with the paperwork. This is not personal. It's regulatory avoidance.

### Your Best Options

**1. Charles Schwab High Yield Investor Checking** — The gold standard for US nomads
- Zero foreign transaction fees, ALL ATM surcharges worldwide reimbursed
- No monthly fees, no minimum balance
- Full mobile banking, excellent customer service
- **Open this BEFORE you leave the US.** It's much harder to open from abroad. Apply online at schwab.com — you'll get a brokerage account bundled with the checking account

**2. Wise (US account)** — Multi-currency workhorse
- Hold and convert 40+ currencies at mid-market rates
- Low transfer fees (typically 0.43-0.6%)
- Physical debit card for purchases and ATM use
- Good for receiving payments in multiple currencies if you freelance

**3. Mercury** — If you have a US business
- Business banking designed for startups and remote companies
- Works well internationally, good API integrations
- No monthly fees, no minimum balance
- Solid option if you're a freelancer with an LLC

### Opening a Thai Bank Account as an American

**Bangkok Bank** is the most American-friendly Thai bank. They have a correspondent banking relationship with US banks and are more accustomed to FATCA compliance.

**What you'll need:**
- Passport
- Valid visa (non-immigrant strongly preferred; DTV results vary by branch)
- A letter from the US Embassy in Bangkok ($50 service fee) — this is essentially a letter confirming you live in Thailand. Call the embassy at +66 2 205 4000 to schedule
- Proof of Thai address (rental contract)
- Thai phone number

**Success rate:** About 60-70% with all documents. If one branch says no, try another branch — policies are enforced inconsistently. Main branches in expat areas (Sukhumvit, Silom) have the highest success rates.

### FBAR Filing — Don't Skip This

If your foreign financial accounts (including Thai bank accounts, Wise multi-currency balances, or any non-US account) exceed **$10,000 at ANY point during the year** — even for a single day — you must file **FinCEN Form 114 (FBAR)**.

- **Deadline:** April 15 (automatic extension to October 15)
- **Filed electronically** at bsaefiling.fincen.treas.gov
- **Penalties for non-filing:** Up to $10,000 per violation (per account, per year) for non-willful violations. Willful violations can reach $100,000 or 50% of account balance
- This is separate from your regular tax return. Don't confuse FBAR with FATCA Form 8938 (which is filed WITH your tax return and has higher thresholds)

**Bottom line for US nomads:** Open Schwab and Wise before you leave. Use Schwab for ATM withdrawals (fees reimbursed) and Wise for transfers and card payments. File your FBAR every year. Don't let FATCA anxiety stop you — it's manageable once you understand the system.

## Opening a Thai Bank Account

### Can You Open One?

**It's gotten harder since 2024.** Most banks now want a non-immigrant visa. Tourist visas and visa exemptions are usually rejected. DTV holders have mixed results — depends on the branch.

### Best Banks for Foreigners

| Bank | Foreigner-Friendly | Best App | Notes |
|------|-------------------|----------|-------|
| **Bangkok Bank** | Most consistent | OK | Largest international network |
| **Kasikorn (KBank)** | Good in expat areas | **K PLUS** (excellent) | Best mobile banking |
| **SCB** | Good for Elite Visa | SCB EASY | Wide ATM network |

### What You Need

- Passport (6+ months validity)
- Valid visa (non-immigrant preferred)
- Proof of Thai address (lease agreement)
- Thai phone number
- Initial deposit: 500-2,000 THB

### Tips for Success

- Go to a **main branch in an expat area** (Sukhumvit, Nimman, Phuket Town)
- **Weekday mornings** when staff aren't rushed
- Dress neatly — it matters
- If rejected, try another branch. Don't argue, just move on.
- Having a letter from your employer increases success rate

## Digital Payments

### PromptPay (Thailand's Instant Payment)

Used everywhere — from 7-Eleven to street vendors. QR code based.

- **With Thai bank account:** Register your phone number via bank app. Works instantly.
- **Without Thai bank account:** You can't scan PromptPay QR codes directly.
- **Coming May 2026:** Wise will integrate PromptPay — game-changer for nomads.

### Credit Card Acceptance

| Venue | Card Accepted? |
|-------|---------------|
| Hotels, malls, chain restaurants | Yes (95%+) |
| Independent restaurants (tourist areas) | Usually (70%) |
| 7-Eleven, convenience stores | Yes (90%+) |
| Grab (taxi/food) | Yes (in-app) |
| Street food, markets | Rarely (cash/QR only) |
| Tuk-tuks, traditional taxis | Cash only |

**Rule of thumb:** Carry 1,000-2,000 THB cash at all times for street food, markets, and small shops.

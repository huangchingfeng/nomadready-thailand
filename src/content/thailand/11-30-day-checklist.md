# Bonus: 30-Day Action Checklist

> **Last Updated:** March 2026
> **Purpose:** A step-by-step playbook from pre-departure to fully settled. Print this. Check things off. Don't overthink it.

---

## Phase 0: Before Departure (2 Weeks Before)

### Visa & Documents
- [ ] Determine your visa type (see Chapter 1). For most nomads: DTV or visa exemption for your first trip
- [ ] If applying for DTV: submit application at thaievisa.go.th (allow 5-15 working days)
- [ ] Confirm passport has 6+ months validity from your entry date
- [ ] Complete Thailand Digital Arrival Card (TDAC) within 72 hours before flight — tdac.immigration.go.th
- [ ] Get an International Driving Permit (IDP) if you plan to ride a motorbike — do this at home, it's harder to get abroad
- [ ] Make 2 color photocopies of your passport (photo page + visa page). Store separately from your passport
- [ ] Register with your country's embassy in Thailand (e.g., STEP for US citizens, Smartraveller for Australians)

### Insurance
- [ ] Purchase travel/health insurance that covers Thailand. Verify it covers:
  - [ ] Motorbike accidents (only if you have a valid motorcycle license — most policies exclude this otherwise)
  - [ ] Emergency medical evacuation
  - [ ] COVID-19 and infectious diseases
  - [ ] Personal liability
  - [ ] Electronics and laptop coverage (optional but recommended)
- [ ] Recommended providers: SafetyWing (nomad-specific), World Nomads, Insured Nomads, Genki
- [ ] Save your insurance policy number and emergency contact in your phone AND email it to yourself

### Money
- [ ] Notify your bank you'll be using your card in Thailand (to prevent fraud blocks)
- [ ] Get a travel-friendly debit card with low/no foreign transaction fees (Wise card, Revolut, Charles Schwab)
- [ ] Download the Wise app for international transfers at good rates
- [ ] Have at least $300-500 USD equivalent in cash (Thai baht or USD) for your first 48 hours — airport exchange rates are decent at Suvarnabhumi
- [ ] Know the proof-of-funds requirement: 20,000 THB (~$570) per person for tourist visa/visa-exempt entry (rarely checked but can be)

### Tech Setup
- [ ] Download these apps BEFORE you land:
  - [ ] **LINE** (create account now — it's the #1 communication app in Thailand)
  - [ ] **Grab** (ride-hailing + food delivery)
  - [ ] **Bolt** (backup ride-hailing)
  - [ ] **Google Maps** (download Thailand offline map: Google Maps > Your profile > Offline maps)
  - [ ] **Google Translate** (download Thai language pack for offline use)
  - [ ] **XE Currency** (currency converter)
  - [ ] **Agoda** (accommodation booking)
  - [ ] **IQAir** (air quality — essential for Chiang Mai)
  - [ ] **12Go** (train/bus/ferry booking)
- [ ] If your phone supports eSIM: purchase a TrueMove H Tourist eSIM online (~$9.90 for 10 days) — activate on landing
- [ ] Back up your laptop and phone
- [ ] Set up 2-factor authentication on all important accounts
- [ ] Enable "Find My Device" on phone and laptop
- [ ] VPN: Download and test a VPN app (ExpressVPN, NordVPN, or Surfshark). Some services may be blocked or throttled

### Packing Essentials (Thailand-Specific)
- [ ] Lightweight, breathable clothing (it's hot and humid year-round)
- [ ] One modest outfit for temple visits (covers shoulders + knees)
- [ ] Flip-flops/sandals (you'll remove shoes constantly)
- [ ] Rain jacket or compact umbrella (year-round useful)
- [ ] Universal power adapter (Thailand uses Type A, B, C, and O outlets — a universal adapter covers all)
- [ ] Sunscreen SPF 50+ (expensive in Thailand, bring from home)
- [ ] Insect repellent with DEET
- [ ] Basic medicine kit: ibuprofen, anti-diarrheal, rehydration salts, allergy meds, any prescriptions
- [ ] N95/KN95 masks (especially if going to Chiang Mai between Feb-Apr)
- [ ] Padlock (for hostel lockers, if applicable)
- [ ] Photocopy of passport (stored separately from original)
- [ ] Physical backup of insurance details

### Getting There from South America

There are no direct flights from South America to Thailand. Every route requires at least one connection, but the Middle Eastern carriers offer the best experience and value.

**Best Routes:**

| Origin | Route | Airlines | Total Time | Round Trip Cost |
|--------|-------|----------|-----------|----------------|
| **GRU (São Paulo)** | Via Dubai (DXB) or Doha (DOH) | Emirates, Qatar Airways | 20-28 hours | $800-1,500 |
| **GRU (São Paulo)** | Via Abu Dhabi (AUH) | Etihad | 22-28 hours | $850-1,400 |
| **GIG (Rio de Janeiro)** | Via Dubai or Doha | Emirates, Qatar | 22-30 hours | $850-1,600 |
| **EZE (Buenos Aires)** | Via Dubai or Doha | Emirates, Qatar | 24-30 hours | $900-1,600 |
| **BOG (Bogotá)** | Via Seoul (ICN) or Tokyo (NRT) | Korean Air, ANA | 22-28 hours | $900-1,500 |
| **MEX (Mexico City)** | Via Tokyo (NRT) or Seoul (ICN) | ANA, Korean Air | 20-26 hours | $800-1,400 |

**Tips for booking from South America:**
- **Book 2-3 months in advance** for the best prices. Peak season (December-January) prices jump 30-50%
- **Qatar Airways and Emirates** have the best connections from South America to Southeast Asia — frequent flights, good layover times, quality service
- **Consider a 1-day stopover** in Dubai or Doha to break the journey. Both airports have excellent transit hotels, and a day to stretch your legs makes the trip much more manageable
- **One-way tickets** are often only slightly more than half the round-trip price. If you're not sure when you'll return, book one-way and buy the return later
- **Google Flights** (google.com/flights) is the best tool for comparing routes and tracking prices. Set a price alert for your route

### Accommodation (Book First 3-7 Nights Only)
- [ ] Book accommodation for your first 3-7 nights only. You'll want to see areas in person before committing
- [ ] Recommended platforms: Agoda (best Asia prices), Booking.com, Airbnb
- [ ] First-timer suggestion:
  - **Bangkok:** Sukhumvit area (On Nut to Thong Lo) — near BTS, food, coworking
  - **Chiang Mai:** Nimman area — walkable, cafes, coworking, nomad-central
- [ ] Budget: 500-1,500 THB/night for a clean hotel/guesthouse; 800-2,000 THB/night for Airbnb with kitchen

## Week 1: Landing & Setup (Days 1-7)

### Day 1: Arrival

#### Your First Hours in Bangkok (BKK Airport)

This is the step-by-step for Suvarnabhumi Airport (BKK), where most international flights land. If you're nervous — don't be. Thousands of nomads do this every week.

**Immigration:**
- [ ] Have these ready BEFORE you reach the officer: passport, TDAC confirmation (on your phone is fine), hotel booking confirmation, and return/onward flight ticket
- [ ] The officer may ask: "How long are you staying?" and "Where are you staying?" Simple, short answers are fine. Don't volunteer extra information
- [ ] DTV holders: have your e-Visa approval printout or screenshot ready. The officer may not be familiar with DTV — stay calm, it's valid

**SIM Card:**
- [ ] Exit the arrivals hall, turn right. You'll see AIS and TrueMove H counters immediately — they're impossible to miss
- [ ] Tourist SIM: ~300 THB for 30 days with 30GB+ data. You need your passport (they'll register it on the spot)
- [ ] AIS has the best nationwide coverage; TrueMove H is a solid alternative
- [ ] Skip this step if you activated an eSIM before landing

**Cash:**
- [ ] ATM machines are in the arrivals hall. Withdraw 5,000-10,000 THB for your first few days of expenses
- [ ] Use **AEON ATMs** if you can find one (lowest foreign card fee: 150 THB). They're in the basement level near the Airport Rail Link entrance
- [ ] Avoid yellow Krungsri ATMs (250 THB fee) — they're the most expensive
- [ ] **Always select "WITHOUT CONVERSION"** when the ATM asks about currency — selecting your home currency costs 3-5% extra
- [ ] Alternative: exchange a small amount of cash ($100-200) at the airport exchange counters — rates at Suvarnabhumi are reasonable

**Transport to City:**
- [ ] **Airport Rail Link to Phaya Thai** (45 THB, 30 min) → connect to BTS Skytrain. Best value, fast, no traffic. Runs 6:00 AM - midnight
- [ ] **Grab taxi** (app): ~300-500 THB to central Bangkok. Book from the app, meet your driver at the pickup point (Level 2). Air-conditioned, fixed price, no surprises
- [ ] **Official taxi meter**: ~250-400 THB to central Bangkok + 50 THB airport surcharge + highway tolls (~25-75 THB). Join the taxi queue on Level 1
- [ ] **DO NOT take an unmetered taxi.** If a driver says "no meter" or quotes a flat rate — walk away. Always insist on the meter. There's always another taxi
- [ ] **From Don Mueang (DMK):** Grab (~200-350 THB) or A1/A2 bus to BTS Mo Chit (30 THB)
- [ ] **From Chiang Mai (CNX):** Grab (~150-200 THB to Nimman/Old City)
- [ ] Avoid any tuk-tuk driver or "taxi" tout who approaches you inside the terminal. Legitimate drivers don't solicit inside

**First Meal:**
- [ ] Hungry? 7-Eleven is in the arrivals hall — sandwiches, onigiri, and drinks for 30-80 THB
- [ ] For a real meal: take the elevator to the **4th floor food court** — it's significantly cheaper than the ground floor restaurants. A plate of pad Thai or rice with curry: 80-150 THB

**At Your Accommodation:**
- [ ] Test the WiFi speed (speedtest.net). You need 20+ Mbps download for stable video calls
- [ ] Check the air conditioning works properly
- [ ] Photograph the room condition (for deposit protection if applicable)
- [ ] Locate the nearest 7-Eleven (usually within 200 meters — they're everywhere)
- [ ] Buy water, basic snacks, and any toiletries you need

### Days 2-3: Explore Your Neighborhood

- [ ] Walk a 1 km radius around your accommodation. Find:
  - [ ] The nearest BTS/MRT station (Bangkok) or songthaew route (Chiang Mai)
  - [ ] 2-3 local food spots (look for places with Thai customers — that's where the good food is)
  - [ ] A pharmacy (look for the green cross sign)
  - [ ] A laundry shop (ubiquitous, 30-50 THB/kg)
  - [ ] An ATM inside a bank branch (safer than standalone ATMs)
  - [ ] The nearest coworking space
- [ ] Try your first street food meal. Pad Thai, som tam (papaya salad), or khao man gai (chicken rice) are safe first choices
- [ ] Get your first Grab ride to get comfortable with the app
- [ ] Walk through the nearest market (morning markets have the best food)

### Days 4-5: Digital Setup

- [ ] Visit a coworking space for a day pass. Test the space and start meeting people
  - **Chiang Mai:** Punspace Nimman, Yellow, or Alt_CM
  - **Bangkok:** The Hive (Thong Lo or Sathorn), Hubba, or True Digital Park
- [ ] Ask coworking staff for local WhatsApp/LINE group invite links
- [ ] Post an introduction in one Facebook group: "Just arrived in [city], [your profession], looking to connect. Anyone want to grab coffee?"
- [ ] Set up Grab for food delivery (GrabFood) — you'll use this on busy work days
- [ ] Download LINE MAN for local Thai food delivery options
- [ ] If you need a Thai phone number for banking or other services later, confirm your SIM card has one (tourist SIMs usually do)

### Days 6-7: First Social Steps

- [ ] Attend your first meetup or social event
  - Check Meetup.com, Facebook groups, or ask at your coworking space for upcoming events
  - The Nomad Coffee Club (Chiang Mai) is the easiest first step — just show up with a laptop
- [ ] Exchange LINE contacts with at least 3 people
- [ ] Start learning basic Thai phrases (see Chapter 9). Even "sawasdee krap/ka" and "kop khun krap/ka" will change how people treat you
- [ ] If you arrived during burning season (Feb-Apr) in Chiang Mai: check IQAir daily. If AQI exceeds 150+, stay indoors or consider relocating south

**End of Week 1 checkpoint:** You should have a working phone, a neighborhood you know, a coworking space you've tested, and at least a few social contacts.

## Week 2: Getting Settled (Days 8-14)

### Accommodation Decision

- [ ] If your first booking was a hotel/Airbnb test run, now search for monthly accommodation:
  - **Facebook groups** — Post "looking for monthly condo in [area]" in local nomad groups. You'll get 20 recommendations
  - **Walk-in** — Visit condo buildings in your preferred area and ask the reception about monthly rates. This often gets you 20-40% below online prices
  - **Platforms:** Hipflat, DDproperty, Facebook Marketplace
  - **Budget:**
    - Chiang Mai: 5,000-12,000 THB/month (studio/1BR condo)
    - Bangkok: 8,000-20,000 THB/month (studio/1BR condo near BTS)
    - Phuket: 8,000-18,000 THB/month
  - **Negotiate:** Monthly rates are almost always negotiable, especially for 2-3 month commitments
- [ ] Before signing: check WiFi speed, AC condition, building security, proximity to BTS/coworking
- [ ] Photograph everything on move-in day (walls, floors, furniture, appliances)
- [ ] Send photos to landlord via LINE and ask them to confirm the condition — this protects your deposit
- [ ] Verify your landlord files TM30 with immigration (required within 24 hours of moving in). Hotels do this automatically; private landlords often don't. Ask directly

### Work Routine

- [ ] Commit to a coworking space (day passes or weekly/monthly membership)
- [ ] Establish your daily work schedule. Account for time zone differences with clients/employer
- [ ] If working US hours from Thailand: You'll be working evenings/nights (12-13 hour difference). Many nomads adjust by working 6 PM - 2 AM and having mornings free
- [ ] If working European hours: Much easier — 5-7 hour difference means you work afternoons
- [ ] Test a video call from your coworking space to confirm quality
- [ ] Set up a backup work location (a cafe with good WiFi, or a second coworking space) for days your primary spot is full or has issues

### Health & Fitness

- [ ] Locate the nearest hospital. Recommended chains:
  - **Bangkok:** Bumrungrad, BNH, Samitivej
  - **Chiang Mai:** Chiang Mai Ram, Lanna Hospital
  - **Phuket:** Bangkok Hospital Phuket, Siriroj Hospital
- [ ] Visit a gym or try your first Muay Thai class
  - Gym day pass: 150-300 THB
  - Muay Thai drop-in: 300-600 THB
- [ ] Start a fitness routine. Exercise is one of the best ways to stay healthy, meet people, and manage the mental health challenges of nomad life
- [ ] If you take prescription medication: visit a Thai pharmacy. Many prescription medications are available over-the-counter in Thailand at a fraction of the cost. Bring your prescription paperwork from home

### Banking (If Staying Long-Term)

- [ ] **Short-term (1-3 months):** A Thai bank account is NOT necessary. Use Wise card + ATM withdrawals
- [ ] **Long-term (3+ months on a proper visa):** Consider opening a Thai bank account
  - **Easiest banks for foreigners:** Kasikorn Bank (K-Bank) or Bangkok Bank
  - **Requirements (vary by branch):** Passport, visa, proof of address (rental contract), work permit or school confirmation (if applicable)
  - **Important (January 2026 update):** Bangkok Bank now requires Non-B, Non-O, ED, LTR, or retirement extension visa. Tourist and DTV visa holders can no longer open accounts there. Kasikorn remains more flexible
  - **Minimum deposit:** 500-2,000 THB
  - **Why bother:** Thai bank transfers (PromptPay) are free and instant. Many local services and payments prefer or require Thai bank transfers

## Week 3: Community (Days 15-21)

### Deepen Your Social Network

- [ ] Attend at least 2 different types of events this week:
  - One professional (coworking social, startup meetup, tech talk)
  - One social (dinner group, sports activity, language exchange)
- [ ] Accept a social invitation from someone you met in Weeks 1-2. Say yes to coffee, dinner, or a day trip
- [ ] Organize something small yourself — a lunch with 3-4 people, a coffee meetup, a weekend trip. Taking initiative accelerates friendships
- [ ] If in Chiang Mai: join a WhatsApp activity group (hiking, fitness, AI/tech, etc.)
- [ ] If in Bangkok: join a LINE community group for your area

### Food Adventures

- [ ] Try a Thai cooking class (half-day: 800-1,500 THB). Great for meeting people and learning to cook Thai food
- [ ] Explore a new neighborhood specifically for food:
  - **Bangkok:** Chinatown (Yaowarat) for street food, Ari for hipster cafes, Chatuchak for weekend market food
  - **Chiang Mai:** Old City for cheap local eats, Santitham for hip restaurants, local morning markets for authentic Thai breakfast
- [ ] Visit a night market:
  - **Bangkok:** Jodd Fairs (Rama 9) or Rot Fai (Ratchada)
  - **Chiang Mai:** Sunday Walking Street (Ratchadamnoen Road) or Saturday Night Market (Wualai Road)
- [ ] Order from LINE MAN to discover local restaurants with Wongnai reviews

### Explore Beyond Your Area

- [ ] Do a day trip:
  - **From Bangkok:** Ayutthaya temples, Amphawa floating market, or Khao Yai National Park
  - **From Chiang Mai:** Doi Inthanon, Elephant Nature Park, or Sticky Waterfalls
- [ ] Visit a major temple properly:
  - **Bangkok:** Wat Pho (Reclining Buddha), Wat Arun (Temple of Dawn)
  - **Chiang Mai:** Wat Phra That Doi Suthep (mountain temple — take the red songthaew up)
  - Remember: dress modestly (cover shoulders + knees), remove shoes, be respectful

### Administrative Tasks

- [ ] If staying 90+ days: Set a reminder for 90-day reporting (TM47) — must be done 15 days before or 7 days after the 90-day mark
- [ ] Confirm your TM30 was filed (ask your landlord for the receipt)
- [ ] If your visa allows extension: note the deadline and required documents

## Week 4: Optimized (Days 22-30)

### Evaluate Your Setup

- [ ] **Accommodation:** Are you happy with the location, price, WiFi, and commute? If not, start looking for alternatives. Month-to-month leases make this easy
- [ ] **Coworking:** Is your current space the right fit? Consider trying a different space for variety
- [ ] **Daily routine:** Is your work-life balance working? Adjust your schedule if needed
- [ ] **Budget:** Track your spending for the month. Compare to your expectation:
  - Comfortable budget: 35,000-50,000 THB/month ($1,000-1,400) in Chiang Mai
  - Comfortable budget: 45,000-70,000 THB/month ($1,300-2,000) in Bangkok
  - This covers: rent, food, coworking, transport, gym, social activities
- [ ] **Health:** How's your physical and mental health? Adjust your fitness, diet, and social balance

### The Big Decision

Ask yourself these questions:

1. **Am I productive here?** Is my work output at least as good as before I came?
2. **Am I happy?** Not just "having fun" but genuinely content with daily life?
3. **Do I have a social network?** Can I name 5+ people I'd call to hang out?
4. **Can I afford this?** Is my burn rate sustainable for 3-6 more months?
5. **What's my visa situation?** Do I have enough time on my current visa, or do I need to plan?

**If all answers are positive:** Congratulations. You've found your base. Commit for another 2-3 months. Negotiate a better rate on your condo. Get a monthly coworking membership. Join a gym long-term.

**If some answers are negative:**
- **Productivity issues?** Try a different coworking space, adjust your schedule, or consider whether the city is too distracting
- **Lonely?** Double down on community — attend more events, join a sport, consider a coliving space
- **Too expensive?** Move to a cheaper area or city (Chiang Mai is 30-40% cheaper than Bangkok)
- **Bored?** Consider moving to a different Thai city for your next month. Bangkok → Chiang Mai → Phuket is the classic nomad rotation

### Plan Your Next Move

- [ ] If staying: find longer-term accommodation (3-6 month lease = better rates)
- [ ] If moving to another Thai city: book accommodation for the first week in your next destination
- [ ] If leaving Thailand: check visa exit requirements, overstay penalties, and book your transport
- [ ] Update your 90-day reporting status if applicable
- [ ] Tell your new friends where you're headed — the nomad network is global, and connections follow you

### Final Week Tasks

- [ ] Back up all your data (photos, files, work)
- [ ] Export your LINE chat history with important contacts
- [ ] Write down your top 5 restaurant/cafe/coworking recommendations — you'll be asked for these by every nomad who messages you later
- [ ] If leaving your condo: photograph the condition of the apartment for deposit return. Document everything
- [ ] Leave a review for your accommodation and coworking space — help the next nomad

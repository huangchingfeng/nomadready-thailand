Before you buy any plan, check coverage for:

- **Motorbike accidents:** Only covered if you have a valid motorcycle license AND were wearing a helmet. This is the #1 claim denial in Thailand. Get your International Driving Permit before you leave home.

## EU Citizens: Health Insurance Transition

If you're leaving an EU country for Thailand, your home health insurance situation needs careful planning. This is one of the most common sources of confusion (and expensive mistakes) for European nomads.

### EHIC Card — Does NOT Work in Thailand

Your European Health Insurance Card (EHIC / GHIC for UK) is **only valid in EU/EEA countries and Switzerland.** It provides zero coverage in Thailand. Don't rely on it.

### German Public Insurance (GKV) Transition

If you're on German public health insurance (gesetzliche Krankenversicherung), coverage typically ends after your Abmeldung. Here are your options:

| Option | Cost | Best For |
|--------|------|----------|
| **Anwartschaftsversicherung** | ~55-90 EUR/month | Returning to Germany within 1-3 years. Keeps your GKV membership dormant — immediate re-enrollment when you return |
| **BDAE (Bund der Auslands-Erwerbstätigen)** | Varies by plan | German expats who want German-language customer service and EU-standard coverage while abroad |
| **Allianz Care / Cigna Global** | $150-400+/month | Comprehensive international coverage, strong hospital networks in Thailand |
| **Foyer Global Health** | $100-300+/month | Luxembourg-based, popular with German expats. Good balance of coverage and price |
| **SafetyWing + Anwartschaft combo** | ~$56/4 weeks + ~70 EUR/month | Budget option: emergency coverage in Thailand + keep your German spot |

**Returning to Germany:** If you maintained **Anwartschaft**, re-enrollment in your GKV is immediate — no waiting periods, no health checks. Without it, you may face waiting periods or, if you're **over 55**, you could be permanently locked out of public insurance and forced into private insurance (which gets very expensive with age).

### Other EU Countries — Quick Reference

- **France (Sécurité Sociale):** Coverage ends when you leave. You can maintain **CFE (Caisse des Français de l'Étranger)** for continued French-system coverage abroad — popular but not cheap (~200-400 EUR/month depending on age)
- **Netherlands (Zorgverzekering):** Dutch basic insurance is mandatory while registered in the Netherlands. After deregistration (uitschrijving), coverage ends. You lose any **zorgtoeslag** (healthcare allowance). Re-entry requires purchasing new basic insurance — no waiting periods for basic coverage
- **Spain (Seguridad Social):** Coverage via the national system ends when you deregister (baja). The **Convenio Especial** allows voluntary continued coverage but only within Spain. International coverage requires a private plan

### The Universal Advice for EU Nomads

1. **Don't leave without international health insurance.** Thai hospitals are cheap, but a serious accident or illness can still cost $10,000-50,000+
2. **Decide on Anwartschaft (or your country's equivalent) before you leave.** This decision is much harder to reverse later
3. **Keep records of all medical expenses in Thailand.** Even if you're self-insuring for routine care, you may need these for insurance claims or tax deductions in your home country

## Pharmacy

Thailand pharmacies are **much more relaxed** than Western countries. Many prescription-only medications in the US are available over the counter.

### Available Without Prescription

- Antibiotics (amoxicillin, ciprofloxacin) — 50-200 THB
- Antihistamines (cetirizine, loratadine) — 30-100 THB
- Stomach meds (omeprazole) — 50-150 THB
- Oral contraceptives — 30-200 THB
- Muscle relaxants — 50-150 THB
- Pain relievers (paracetamol, ibuprofen) — 15-80 THB

### Controlled (Prescription Required)

- Opioid painkillers, benzodiazepines, ADHD medications, sleeping pills

### Where to Buy

- **Independent pharmacies** — Cheapest, most flexible
- **Boots / Watsons** — In malls, stricter on prescriptions
- **Hospital pharmacies** — For controlled medications

> 💊 Medications are typically **85-90% cheaper** than the US. A course of antibiotics: $3-5.

## Health Warnings

### Dengue Fever
- Endemic in Thailand. 19,491 cases in 2025.
- Peak: rainy season (June-November)
- **Prevention:** DEET repellent, long sleeves at dawn/dusk, avoid standing water
- **If fever persists 1-2 days:** See a doctor immediately
- **Never take aspirin/ibuprofen** with suspected dengue — use paracetamol only
- Consider Qdenga vaccine (~2,000-3,000 THB/dose at Thai hospitals)

### Air Quality
- **Bangkok:** AQI often 100+ during January-March. Use IQAir app to monitor.
- **Chiang Mai (Feb-April):** AQI 200-300+ during burning season. **Seriously consider leaving.** Many nomads relocate to southern Thailand. See [Chapter 9: Safety & Practical Tips](/guide/09-safety-practical) for seasonal guidance.
- **Best air quality:** June-October (rainy season)

### Food Safety
- Choose **busy stalls** (high turnover = fresher food)
- Bottled/filtered water only — never tap water
- Cylindrical/tubular ice = factory-made (safe). Irregular crushed ice = maybe not.
- Mild stomach issues in first 1-2 weeks are normal
- Carry oral rehydration salts (10 THB at any pharmacy)
